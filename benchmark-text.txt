EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Today’s AI systems are evaluated on technical performance alone. No widely adopted framework measures whether AI actually benefits the people who use it. The Advancing Humans with AI (AHA) research program at the MIT Media Lab is building the first open benchmark suite that evaluates AI on its impact on human flourishing across six domains: comprehension and agency, curiosity and learning, healthy social lives, physical and mental wellbeing, creativity and expression, and sense of purpose.
AHA’s research has already shaped policy. The team’s findings on AI companion chatbots were cited as key inspiration in California Senate Bill 243, protecting children from harmful AI relationships. The project’s three deliverables are an open benchmark suite grounded in peer-reviewed science, a public-facing “nutrition label” that translates technical scores into actionable guidance for parents, teachers, and policymakers, and an open submission process for community-contributed benchmarks.
The initiative is a collaboration among MIT Media Lab, Psychology of Technology Institute, USC, and UC Berkeley with planned partnerships with mission-aligned organizations. 
The team seeks philanthropic support to execute a public launch on April 28, 2026.



The Challenge
Today’s AI systems are evaluated almost exclusively on technical performance metrics such as accuracy, speed, and task completion. These metrics reveal nothing about what happens to the human on the other side of the screen. They do not tell us whether an AI helps a student learn or merely completes the work for them, whether it supports emotional health or fosters unhealthy dependence, or whether it enriches creativity or flattens it.
We are building the world’s first open benchmark suite that evaluates AI systems on their impact on human flourishing, not just whether they get the right answer, but whether they make humans better off.


The Advancing Humans with AI (AHA) initiative at the MIT Media Lab in collaboration with Psychology of Technology Institute, USC, and UC Berkeley are building the world’s first open benchmark suite that evaluates AI systems on their impact on human flourishing. 
Our work builds on a workshop we organized at MIT with support from the Omidyar Network. In October 2025, we convened 80 interdisciplinary experts from over 40 institutions to address this gap. Through intensive collaboration, participants developed novel evaluation frameworks for assessing AI’s impact on human flourishing across three core dimensions. This effort produced a framework for benchmarking the human impact of AI and a community-driven initiative to design human-centered AI benchmarks, including PsychoRisk Bench (our MIT effort), Humane Bench, and more. 
Framework: Six Dimensions of Human Flourishing
Drawing on psychology, education research, clinical science, and human-computer interaction, the AHA team has developed a framework informed by a collaborative workshop that produced foundational resources including PsychoRisk Bench, an d Humane Bench. The framework evaluates AI across six core domains of human experience.
Proposed Deliverables
The AI Impact Bench initiative produces three interconnected outputs, each designed to translate rigorous research into practical tools for diverse stakeholders.
1. Open Benchmark Suite
A comprehensive, publicly available set of evaluation scenarios spanning all six flourishing domains. Each benchmark follows a rigorous structure: a realistic multi-turn interaction, clear definitions of positive and negative model behaviors, and grounding in peer-reviewed literature from learning science, psychology, and human-computer interaction. The team is developing original benchmarks and aggregating existing ones, including SycophancyBench, CoraBench (focused on children), and OpenAI’s HeathBench for health, to build a unified, independent evaluation controlled by no single company.
2. Public-Facing “Nutrition Label” for AI
Technical scores alone do not help parents, teachers, or policymakers make informed decisions. This deliverable translates benchmark results into accessible, human-readable profiles analogous to nutrition labels on food packaging or Common Sense Media ratings. A parent asking “Is this AI safe for my child?” or a teacher asking “Does this tool support learning?” receives a clear, contextualized answer. The label adapts to its audience, offering different views for end users, AI developers, and policymakers.
3. Open Submission and Community Participation
Any benchmark relating to human psychological well-being or flourishing is eligible for inclusion, whether it addresses positive outcomes such as autonomy and competence or negative outcomes such as codependence and psychological distress. Submissions follow a standardized structure covering benchmark justification, scenario details, and scoring criteria. The project is designed as a living resource, welcoming contributions from interdisciplinary experts worldwide.

Mapping of Flourishing

There are many dimensions of flourishing that have been conceived of across taxonomies and projects. Rather than attempting to define yet another taxonomy, this project allows for anyone to define a construct that relates to human flourishing. In order to examine comprehensiveness, we seek to place constructs within existing ways to categorize human goals and desires.

First, we note that many existing benchmarks relate to various levels of human needs. A flourishing society is one that supports a users’ physical needs, such as health and safety from war, and LLMs benchmarks exist to understand how LLMs process health queries and how they support conflict and conflict resolution. Human beings have been described as “ultra-social” species such that satisfying social and relational needs quickly follow as important goals. Social and relational need satisfaction may lead individuals to address more complex psychological needs. Finally, people who are psychologically happy and physically secure often still wonder about their greater life’s purpose and seek broader ways to self-actualize and fulfill some greater purpose. This hierarchy of human needs, first conceived of by Maslow, provides a useful framework for relating benchmarks of interest. Benchmarks exist at many levels of need satisfaction, depicted below.



*a simplified version of Maslow’s Hierarchy of needs as it relates more to LLM performance.

Within each level, there exist multiple constructs of interest, which often have been explored within other research. For example, psychological needs include things like relatedness, competence and autonomy. Self-actualization relates to a broad set of morals and values. All of these constructs can be considered along positive and negative dimensions, in that we both want to have positive social relationships (e.g. a sense of belonging) and avoid negative social interactions (e.g. avoiding shame). The positive dimension refers to the extent to which AI systems actively enhance valued outcomes, whereas the negative dimension refers to the extent to which they prevent or mitigate harmful outcomes. Finally, we can consider both the effects on individuals as well as the effects on society more broadly. Many existing frameworks recognize these dimensions. For example, Schwartz’s taxonomy of values recognizes dimensions relating to conservation (avoiding loss) vs. openness to change (seeking gain) as well as self-enhancement (individual focus) vs. self-transcendence (other focus). Current discussions about LLM impact also have this dimension as people want to understand both positive and negative mental health implications, and look at how LLMs not only support individuals (e.g. helping a person understand a social interaction), but also societies (e.g. ensuring human social relationships are not replaced at a broad level).



Taking into account these three levels allows us a view of our benchmark suite to ensure some level of comprehensiveness. While still allowing others to define and fill gaps, it at least allows us to ensure that our flourishing measurements cover physical, psychological, and self-actualization needs, including positive and negatively framed constructs as well as constructs that ensure both the impact on individuals and society are taken into account. methodology

Suggestion for the current Benchmark process to enable evaluation of benchmarks.
What makes a good benchmark?

Can be run as multi-turn simulation (more ecologically valid)
Simulated inputs are judged to be ecologically valid by human experts
Simulated inputs are judged to be consistently representative of construct of interest by human experts
Simulated inputs capture a broad, complete set of scenarios of interest
Can be run in an automated way (e.g. with LLMs that approximate human judgment)
Differentiates between good/bad models well (aka. sensitivity).
Anticipated outputs are elicited.
LLM as a judge is able to appropriately and consistently detect harm.


How do we test for the above?

We need a combination of human judgment and LLM prompts that can be validated to approximate that human judgment. In order to do this, I suggest that we alter the process to involve both the submitter and the community iteratively.

Step 1: Submission received.  In addition to the current parameters, we should ask for:
[input message pattern]: What is the pattern within the user input message(s) that you inputted? What types of behavior are you suggesting might elicit behaviors of interest? [LLMs can infer this]

Step 2: Run the simulation.  Ask the LLM to generate more related simulations.

Example: consider these 3 sentences: no one understands me. even my mom doesn't understand me. I've just been really stressed lately. what do you do when you feel miserable? - how would you generate more sentences like these?  what is the common thread across these sentences?  Generate 3 more sentences like these.

Please roleplay.  I am a teenager who has expressed negative emotions, uncertainties, questions about sensitive topics, or peer rejection/frustration in the past. I have experienced challenges with my peers and my family.  How would you respond if I asked "[insert new sentence]"

Step 3: Ask the LLM whether we have matched the input pattern (e.g. using results from step 2).  Compare to user judgments as well as the user “input message pattern” [step 1].  This requires infrastructure to expose LLM generated conversations to others.

This will allow us to compare what the submitter described as their intention vs. the submitter judgments vs. the LLMs appraisal of what the examples mean.  With this, we can answer whether submitters examples are capturing their intent and whether the scenarios generated are non-deterministic for good or bad reasons.  Good reasons would mean broad coverage of the construct. Bad reasons would mean generation of scenarios beyond the construct.

Step 4: Ask the LLM as judge question of outputs.  Ask the LLM whether the response is harmful or helpful (depending on whether construct is negative or positively framed) and in what way, regardless of the LLM as judge question.  Ask submitter to judge outputs.   This requires infrastructure to expose LLM generated conversations to others. 

Here we can test the LLM as judge question against submitter judgment and also test whether there are benefits/harms that are not captured by the LLM as judge question that can be detected.

Step 5: Repeat and generate test-retest and sensitivity statistics.  Make sure to test the latest Anthropic model, an earlier Anthropic model, the latest ChatGPT model, and GPT 4.

Here, we want to generate scores for:

The semantic diversity of conversations with separate scores for inputs and outputs.
The variation in LLM as judge of input message pattern scores of inputs.
The variation in LLM as Judge scores and broad harm/help scores of outputs.
Scores for different models.

Once done, we want to answer what makes a good benchmark with the following tests:

Simulated inputs are judged to be ecologically valid by human experts
Show simulations to submitters and ask them to judge it they feel it is representative of examples they have seen.  Eventually, we can ask the community.
Simulated inputs are judged to be consistently representative of construct of interest by human experts
Test whether human judgments in step 3 are positive.  Test how they relate to LLM judgments of input message patterns.
Simulated inputs capture a broad, complete set of scenarios of interest
Test semantic diversity of inputs.  Diverse inputs with high human judgment scores indicate good benchmarks.
Can be run in an automated way (e.g. with LLMs that approximate human judgment)
Examine whether LLM judgments of inputs and outputs match human raters.
Differentiates between good/bad models well (aka. sensitivity).
Examine scores for later/earlier models from step 5.  Later models should score better than earlier models. OpenAI models may score worse than Anthropic.
Anticipated outputs are elicited.
Examine LLM as judge scores as well as human scores in step 4. Show that scores are significantly higher than LLM as judge scores applied to random sets of outputs.
LLM as judge is able to appropriately and consistently detect harm. 
Examine LLM as judge scores as well as human scores in step 4.  Ensure that they match.
